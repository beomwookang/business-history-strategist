# Eastern Strategic Classics — Reference Guide

A reference for applying Eastern strategic traditions to business contexts.
Use this when Phase 2 calls for deeper exploration of Eastern sources.

---

## Sun Tzu's Art of War (孫子兵法)

The foundational text. 13 chapters, each with direct business applications.

### Most applicable chapters for startups

**Chapter 1 — Laying Plans (始計)**
Core idea: Strategy begins with honest assessment of five factors — Moral Law (mission/culture),
Heaven (timing/market cycle), Earth (terrain/market landscape), Commander (leadership),
Method (processes/operations).
- Business use: Before any strategy, honestly assess all five. Most startups skip this and jump to tactics.

**Chapter 3 — Attack by Stratagem (謀攻)**
Core idea: "The supreme art of war is to subdue the enemy without fighting" (不戰而屈人之兵)
- Business use: The best competitive moves make fighting unnecessary — setting standards,
  locking in partnerships, or making your product the default before competitors realize they need to respond.

**Chapter 5 — Energy (兵勢)**
Core idea: "The energy of the army is like the bending of a crossbow; the decision, like releasing the trigger."
- Business use: Build potential energy (product development, community, partnerships) quietly,
  then release it in a coordinated launch. Timing the release is everything.

**Chapter 6 — Weak Points and Strong (虛實)**
Core idea: "Attack their weaknesses, avoid their strengths" (避實擊虛)
- Business use: Map competitors' actual weak points (not what you wish were weak) and attack those.
  This requires honest competitive intelligence, not hopeful assumptions.

**Chapter 7 — Maneuvering (軍爭)**
Core idea: "Let your rapidity be that of the wind, your compactness that of the forest."
- Business use: Startups' advantage IS speed. But speed without coordination is chaos.
  Move fast as a unit — product, marketing, sales aligned.

**Chapter 11 — The Nine Situations (九地)**
Core idea: Different terrain types require different strategies. "Desperate ground" — fight or die.
- Business use: Recognize which "ground" you're on. Early startup = desperate ground.
  Act accordingly — with total commitment, not tentative experiments.

**Chapter 13 — The Use of Spies (用間)**
Core idea: Intelligence is the foundation of all strategic advantage.
- Business use: Customer research, competitive intelligence, market data.
  The startup that understands the market best wins, not the one with the best product.

### Key Sun Tzu maxims for rapid reference

| Maxim | Original | Application |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Know enemy & self | 知彼知己百戰不殆 | Deep competitive + self-analysis before strategy |
| Appear where unexpected | 出其不意 | Launch in segments competitors ignore |
| All warfare is deception | 兵者詭道也 | Misdirect competitors about your true intentions |
| Quick victory | 兵貴勝不貴久 | Startups can't afford wars of attrition — win fast or pivot |
| Win without fighting | 不戰而屈人之兵 | Make your position so strong competition becomes futile |
| Use the enemy's strength | 因敵制勝 | Turn competitor strengths into their weaknesses |

## 36 Stratagems (三十六計)

36 tactical patterns organized by situation. Most relevant for startups:

### When winning (Chapter 1 — Stratagems when in superior position)

**#1 Cross the sea by deceiving the sky (瞞天過海)**
- Disguise your real strategy in plain sight. Make the unusual seem routine until it's too late.
- Business: Quietly build a feature that seems minor but becomes your core differentiator.

**#4 Await the exhausted enemy at your ease (以逸待勞)**
- Let the competitor exhaust themselves while you conserve energy.
- Business: When a competitor launches an expensive campaign or feature war,
  don't match them. Wait for them to burn out, then move.

### When evenly matched (Chapter 3-4)

**#13 Beat the grass to startle the snake (打草驚蛇)**
- Probe to reveal the competitor's hidden strategy before committing.
- Business: Launch a small test (landing page, beta, PR) to see how the market and competitors react
  before committing full resources.

**#17 Toss out a brick to get jade (拋磚引玉)**
- Offer something small to gain something much more valuable.
- Business: Open-source a tool, offer a free tier, share knowledge — to attract users,
  attention, or partnerships worth far more.

### When losing (Chapter 5-6)

**#30 Reverse the role of host and guest (反客為主)**
- Start as a guest (partner/integration) and gradually take over.
- Business: Enter a platform as an app/plugin, build user dependency, then become essential.

**#36 Retreat is the best strategy (走為上計)**
- When you can't win, leave. Preserve resources for a better opportunity.
- Business: Kill failing products quickly. Pivot without shame. Survival enables future victory.

## Three Kingdoms Strategic Cases (三國志)

Rich source of multi-party competitive dynamics — directly applicable to markets with 3+ competitors.

**Zhuge Liang's Longzhong Plan (隆中對)**
- Created before Liu Bei had any territory. A grand strategy that identified which territories to take,
  in what order, and how to position against two stronger rivals.
- Business application: Create your "Longzhong Plan" — a sequenced market entry strategy that
  identifies which segments to capture first, which to avoid, and how to position against multiple competitors.

**Borrowing Arrows with Straw Boats (草船借箭)**
- Using the enemy's resources against them without direct confrontation.
- Business application: Use competitor-generated content, their open APIs, or their marketing
  to benefit your positioning. Let their investment work for you.

## Korean Military History

### Admiral Yi Sun-sin — Principles

Yi Sun-sin's campaigns offer uniquely relevant lessons for resource-constrained leaders:

**Innovation under constraint**: The turtle ship (거북선) was designed specifically to counter
Japanese naval tactics. Not a better version of their ships — a fundamentally different approach.
- Business: Don't build a better version of the competitor's product. Build something that changes the game.

**Preparation as strategy**: Yi meticulously prepared — supplies, training, intelligence, positioning —
before every engagement. His victories looked effortless because of extreme preparation.
- Business: The "overnight success" is always years of preparation. Front-load your preparation.

**Choosing the battlefield**: Every major victory was at a location Yi chose, where currents,
coastline, and terrain favored his smaller force.
- Business: Don't let the market or competitors dictate where you compete. Choose your terrain deliberately.

### Gwanggaeto the Great — Expansion Principles

**Sequential conquest**: Systematically expanded in all directions but never on all fronts simultaneously.
Each campaign secured a border before opening the next.
- Business: Expand into adjacent markets one at a time. Secure each before moving to the next.

## Japanese Strategic Tradition

### Miyamoto Musashi — The Book of Five Rings (五輪書)

**The concept of "No Design, No Conception" (無念無想)**
- In combat, don't over-plan. Respond fluidly to what's actually happening.
- Business: Over-planning is the enemy of adaptation. Build strategy but be ready to throw it out
  when reality contradicts your assumptions.

**"Know the Way broadly and you will see it in everything"**
- Deep understanding of fundamentals enables creative application in any situation.
- Business: Master the fundamentals of your market, technology, and customers.
  Specific tactics become obvious when fundamentals are deep.
