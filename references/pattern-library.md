# Strategic Pattern Library

This file maps common startup/business situations to structurally analogous historical cases.
When analyzing a user's situation, classify it into one or more archetypes, then select the most
relevant historical cases for deeper analysis.

## 1. Resource-Inferior Challenger

**Business situation**: Small startup vs. well-funded incumbent. Limited budget, small team,
but potentially faster and more innovative.

### Historical cases

**Battle of Red Cliffs (적벽대전, 208 AD)**
- Context: Sun Quan & Liu Bei alliance (~50,000) vs. Cao Cao's massive army (~200,000+)
- Key principle: **Coalition of the weak defeats the strong** when the strong is overextended
- Structural elements: Use of terrain (river), exploiting enemy weakness (unfamiliar with naval warfare),
  deception (fire attack), timing (seasonal wind)
- Business mapping:
  - Coalition = strategic partnerships with other small players to counter the dominant player
  - Terrain = choose a battlefield (market segment) where your strengths matter and their strengths don't
  - Overextension = large competitors spread across too many fronts can't focus on your niche
  - Fire attack = a single, decisive product/marketing move that changes the dynamics

**Battle of Myeongnyang (명량해전, 1597)**
- Context: Admiral Yi Sun-sin with 13 ships vs. 133+ Japanese warships
- Key principle: **Extreme terrain leverage** — fight only where geography neutralizes numerical superiority
- Structural elements: Narrow strait with strong currents, forcing enemy into single-file engagement,
  turning the enemy's numbers into a liability
- Business mapping:
  - Narrow strait = a specific niche or distribution channel where being big is actually a disadvantage
  - Current/tide = market forces or platform algorithms you can ride but competitors can't
  - 13 ships = focus all resources on one decisive point rather than spreading thin

**Hannibal at Cannae (216 BC)**
- Context: Carthaginian army (~50,000) vs. larger Roman force (~86,000)
- Key principle: **Tactical innovation overcomes numerical disadvantage**
- Structural elements: Double envelopment, deliberate center retreat to draw enemy in,
  using cavalry superiority on the flanks
- Business mapping:
  - Center retreat = appearing to concede the mainstream market while flanking with adjacent products/services
  - Envelopment = surrounding the competitor's customers by owning the ecosystem around them
  - Cavalry = your fastest, most agile capability — use it decisively, not incrementally

**Sun Tzu — "Attack where they are unprepared, appear where unexpected" (攻其不備, 出其不意)**
- Business application: Don't compete where the incumbent expects you. Find the gap they've neglected
  or the customer segment they've written off. Attack from an angle they don't monitor.

**Clausewitz — "Concentrate forces at the decisive point" (Schwerpunkt)**
- Business application: A startup cannot be good at everything. Identify the ONE capability
  that matters most and pour disproportionate resources into it.

## 2. Market Creation & Blue Ocean

**Business situation**: Creating a new category rather than competing in an existing one.
No direct competitors yet, but need to educate the market and establish legitimacy.

### Historical cases

**Alexander the Great's Eastern Campaign (334-323 BC)**
- Context: Marching into unknown territories, creating a new empire rather than defending existing ones
- Key principle: **Boldness and speed in uncharted territory** — commit fully, don't hedge
- Business mapping:
  - Unknown territory = undefined market category
  - Speed = get to market-defining moments before others realize the opportunity
  - Cultural adaptation = adapting product to local needs as you expand
  - Founding cities = establishing reference customers / use cases that define the category

**Zhuge Liang's "Empty Fort Strategy" (空城計)**
- Context: Appearing strong when actually weak, through sheer confidence and reputation
- Key principle: **Perception creates reality** when you're defining a new space
- Business mapping:
  - Empty fort = projecting market leadership before you have the metrics to back it up
  - Bold signaling = thought leadership, PR, conference keynotes that establish you as the category definer
  - Risk: only works temporarily — you must fill the fort before others call your bluff

**Columbus / Age of Exploration**
- Key principle: **First to find the route owns the trade**
- Business mapping: First to define the category narrative and customer journey captures disproportionate value.
  But explorers who didn't establish colonies lost to those who did — finding the market isn't enough, you must settle it.

## 3. Speed & First-Mover Advantage

**Business situation**: A time-sensitive market opportunity. Either you move fast or someone else captures it.

### Historical cases

**Blitzkrieg (1939-1940)**
- Context: German rapid combined-arms assault bypassing fortified positions
- Key principle: **Speed and coordination break static defenses**
- Business mapping:
  - Bypassing the Maginot Line = skip the incumbent's strength (e.g., their sales force, brand) and attack through a channel they don't control
  - Combined arms = coordinate product, marketing, sales, and partnerships in a single rapid push
  - Encirclement = capture key customers/partners before the competitor can react

**Genghis Khan's Messenger System (Yam)**
- Key principle: **Information speed is strategic advantage**
- Business mapping: The team that learns faster from customers, iterates faster on product,
  and communicates faster internally will outmaneuver larger competitors.

**Napoleon's Central Position Strategy**
- Context: Positioning between two enemy armies to defeat each in turn before they can unite
- Key principle: **Divide and conquer through superior speed**
- Business mapping: If you face two competitors, pick off the weaker one's customers first,
  build strength, then face the stronger one from a position of momentum.

## 4. Defense & Moat Building

**Business situation**: You have early traction or product-market fit. Now you need to make your position defensible
before larger players notice and copy you.

### Historical cases

**Masada & Fortification Doctrine**
- Key principle: **Build layered defenses** — no single wall, but multiple barriers
- Business mapping:
  - Layer 1: Product superiority (keep innovating)
  - Layer 2: Switching costs (data, integrations, workflows)
  - Layer 3: Network effects (value increases with users)
  - Layer 4: Brand/community (emotional loyalty)

**Great Wall of China — Strategic Frontier**
- Key principle: **You can't defend everything; choose what to protect**
- Business mapping: Don't try to defend every feature or segment. Identify your core value
  and build walls around that. Let competitors have the periphery.

**Machiavelli — "A prince must build on good foundations" (The Prince, Ch. 12)**
- Business application: Defensibility comes from structural advantages (technology, data, network),
  not temporary ones (first-mover, pricing). Build the foundation while you have the lead.

## 5. Alliance & Partnership Strategy

**Business situation**: You can't win alone. You need partners, investors, ecosystem players,
or even cooperation with semi-competitors.

### Historical cases

**Warring States — "Horizontal/Vertical Alliances" (合縱連橫)**
- Context: Su Qin's vertical alliance (weak states unite against strong Qin) vs.
  Zhang Yi's horizontal alliance (each weak state allies individually with Qin)
- Key principle: **Alliance architecture determines who wins**
- Business mapping:
  - Vertical (合縱) = multiple small players forming an ecosystem to counter a dominant platform
  - Horizontal (連橫) = partnering directly with the dominant player rather than fighting them
  - The choice depends on whether the dominant player will eventually consume you

**Three Kingdoms — Liu Bei's Alliance Strategy**
- Context: Liu Bei, weakest of three kingdoms, survived through strategic alliances and moral authority
- Key principle: **The weakest player survives by being everyone's preferred partner**
- Business mapping: Be the company everyone wants to integrate with, partner with, or invest in.
  Build reputation for reliability, fairness, and shared value creation.

**NATO / Collective Defense**
- Key principle: **Mutual defense pacts deter aggression**
- Business mapping: Form industry consortiums, open-source communities, or API ecosystems
  where attacking one member means facing the collective.

## 6. Disruption from Below

**Business situation**: You have a simpler, cheaper, or more accessible product that serves
a market the incumbents overlook or consider unprofitable.

### Historical cases

**Guerrilla Warfare — T.E. Lawrence (Lawrence of Arabia)**
- Context: Arab irregulars vs. Ottoman Empire's conventional army
- Key principle: **Avoid pitched battles; erode the enemy's will and resources through asymmetry**
- Business mapping:
  - Don't fight the incumbent where they're strong (enterprise sales, brand, features)
  - Attack where they can't respond efficiently (underserved segments, simpler use cases)
  - Make it expensive for them to chase you down

**Vietnam War — Asymmetric Strategy**
- Key principle: **The weaker side sets the terms of engagement**
- Business mapping: Choose the metrics that matter (speed, user love, community) rather than
  competing on the incumbent's metrics (revenue, market share, features).

**Mao Zedong — "Surround the cities from the countryside" (農村包圍城市)**
- Key principle: **Win the periphery first, then move to the center**
- Business mapping: Start with customers the incumbents don't care about (too small, too niche,
  too price-sensitive). Build capability and reputation there. Expand upward.

**36 Stratagems — "Make a sound in the east, strike in the west" (聲東擊西)**
- Business application: Signal competitive intent in one market to draw the incumbent's attention
  and resources there, while your real growth comes from a different segment or product.

## 7. Pivot & Strategic Retreat

**Business situation**: Current strategy isn't working. Need to change direction without
losing momentum, team morale, or investor confidence.

### Historical cases

**Xenophon's March of the Ten Thousand (Anabasis)**
- Context: 10,000 Greek mercenaries stranded deep in hostile territory after their employer died;
  had to fight their way back to the sea
- Key principle: **A disciplined retreat preserves the army to fight another day**
- Business mapping:
  - Retreat ≠ failure. A controlled pivot that preserves cash, team, and core learnings is strategic.
  - Cut losses on failing products/markets quickly. Don't sink more resources into a lost position.
  - Rally the team around a new shared objective ("reach the sea").

**Kutuzov's Retreat from Moscow (1812)**
- Context: Russian general deliberately retreated, even abandoning Moscow, to overextend Napoleon
- Key principle: **Trading space for time** — give up what doesn't matter to preserve what does
- Business mapping: Sometimes you need to kill a product, exit a market, or shrink the team
  to preserve runway and refocus on what's actually working.

**Sun Tzu — "Know when to fight and when not to fight" (知可以戰與不可以戰者勝)**
- Business application: The best founders know which battles to avoid. Not every competitor
  needs a response. Not every market trend needs chasing.

## 8. Psychological & Perception Warfare

**Business situation**: Shaping how the market, competitors, investors, or media perceive you
is as important as what you actually build.

### Historical cases

**Potemkin Villages — Perception Management**
- Key principle: **Controlled narrative can buy time for reality to catch up**
- Business mapping: Strategic PR, demo videos, partnership announcements, and waitlists
  can create perceived momentum before you have real traction. Use cautiously — credibility is fragile.

**36 Stratagems — "Borrow a corpse to resurrect the soul" (借屍還魂)**
- Business application: Revive an abandoned idea, brand, or technology by reframing it in a new context.
  What failed in 2015 might work now because the market, technology, or timing has changed.

**Napoleon's Bulletins — Controlling the Narrative**
- Key principle: **The story of the battle matters as much as the battle itself**
- Business mapping: How you frame your startup's story (to investors, press, customers)
  shapes how the market treats you. "We're a small team" vs. "We're a focused, elite team"
  describe the same reality but create different perceptions.

## 9. Platform & Ecosystem Dominance

**Business situation**: Building a platform that others build on, or deciding whether to
become a platform vs. stay as an application.

### Historical cases

**Roman Road System**
- Key principle: **Infrastructure becomes the platform for empire**
- Business mapping: Build the infrastructure (APIs, marketplaces, developer tools) that others
  depend on. Once they build on your roads, switching costs become enormous.

**British East India Company — Platform Colonialism**
- Key principle: **Control the trade routes, not the territory**
- Business mapping: You don't need to own every part of the value chain.
  Control the critical chokepoint (distribution, data, user relationship) and tax the flow.

**Gwanggaeto the Great (광개토대왕) — Expansion Strategy**
- Context: Systematic expansion of Goguryeo through both military conquest and tributary systems
- Key principle: **Expand through a mix of direct control and vassal relationships**
- Business mapping:
  - Direct control = own products and features
  - Tributary = partner integrations and marketplace participants
  - The key is knowing which to own and which to partner

## 10. Survival Under Siege

**Business situation**: Running out of runway, losing customers, facing a crisis,
or being actively attacked by a competitor. Pure survival mode.

### Historical cases

**Siege of Leningrad (1941-1944)**
- Context: 872-day siege; city survived through extreme rationing and the "Road of Life" across frozen lake
- Key principle: **Find the one lifeline and protect it at all costs**
- Business mapping:
  - Identify the single revenue stream or customer segment keeping you alive
  - Cut everything else to protect it
  - Find your "Road of Life" — the unconventional channel that sustains you

**Joseon's Righteous Armies (의병) during Japanese Invasions**
- Context: When the official military collapsed, civilian militia organized spontaneously
- Key principle: **Decentralized resilience** — when the formal structure fails, empower the edges
- Business mapping: In crisis, empower individual team members to find solutions.
  Top-down control is too slow. Let customer-facing people make decisions.

**Sun Tzu — "Place your army in deadly peril, and it will survive" (投之亡地然後存)**
- Business application: Sometimes the best move is to embrace the constraint.
  A team with 3 months of runway and no fallback will move with a clarity and urgency
  that a well-funded team never achieves. Use the crisis as fuel, not just a threat.

## Cross-Referencing Guide

Most real situations involve multiple archetypes. Common combinations:

| Situation | Primary | Secondary |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| Bootstrapped startup entering crowded market | #1 Resource-Inferior | #6 Disruption from Below |
| Post-seed startup building defensibility | #4 Defense & Moat | #9 Platform & Ecosystem |
| Failing startup considering pivot | #7 Pivot & Retreat | #10 Survival Under Siege |
| Startup seeking Series A | #8 Perception Warfare | #5 Alliance & Partnership |
| Creating a new product category | #2 Market Creation | #3 Speed & First-Mover |
| Startup being copied by big tech | #1 Resource-Inferior | #4 Defense & Moat |

When multiple archetypes apply, synthesize insights from each rather than treating them separately.
The most powerful strategies often combine principles from 2-3 archetypes.
